Scope of work in Santa Ana

Santa Ana lies within the Peninsular Ranges geomorphic province, underlain by Quaternary alluvial deposits derived from the Santa Ana Mountains and San Gabriel Mountains. The typical soil profile consists of interbedded sands, silts, and clays with variable gravel content, reflecting fluvial and alluvial fan depositional environments. The near-surface soils often include loose to medium dense sands and soft to stiff silty clays, while deeper strata may contain older alluvium or marine terrace deposits. Groundwater is generally encountered at depths of 10 to 30 feet, though it can be shallower near the Santa Ana River or during wet seasons. The region is seismically active, with potential for strong ground shaking from nearby faults such as the San Jacinto and Elsinore fault zones. Liquefaction susceptibility is a concern in saturated, loose granular soils, requiring careful site-specific evaluation using methods like cone penetration testing (ASTM D5778) and cyclic triaxial testing. Our residual soil characterization services help identify the engineering behavior of weathered materials common in hillside areas.

Frequently asked questions

What are the typical soil conditions in Santa Ana for a commercial building?

Santa Ana soils generally consist of interbedded sands, silts, and clays from alluvial deposits. Loose sands and soft clays are common near the surface, requiring careful foundation design. We typically recommend spread footings bearing on engineered fill or deeper foundations like driven piles where liquefiable sands are present. Groundwater is often encountered at 10–30 feet depth, so dewatering or waterproofing may be needed for basements.

How does seismic activity affect foundation design in Santa Ana?

Santa Ana is in a seismically active region with potential for strong ground shaking from nearby faults. Site-specific seismic hazard analysis per ASCE 7-22 is required to determine design ground motions. Liquefaction is a key concern in loose saturated sands, so we evaluate settlement and lateral spreading risks. Mitigation measures may include ground improvement, deep foundations, or soil densification to ensure structural safety.

What geotechnical reports are required for a Santa Ana development permit?

The City of Santa Ana typically requires a geotechnical investigation report prepared under the supervision of a California-licensed geotechnical engineer. The report must include site geology, soil profiles, laboratory test results, foundation recommendations, seismic hazards evaluation, and if needed, slope stability analysis. Reports must comply with CBC Chapter 18 and ASCE 7-22, and are reviewed by the city’s building department during plan check.

Do I need a geotechnical engineer for a small residential addition in Santa Ana?

Yes, even small additions may require a geotechnical evaluation, especially if the project involves new foundations or grading. The city often asks for a letter or report to confirm soil bearing capacity, expansion potential, and seismic site class. We can provide a focused investigation with limited borings and laboratory tests to meet these requirements efficiently, avoiding costly redesigns later.
